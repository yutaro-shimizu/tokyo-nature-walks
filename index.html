The experience

A pocket of ancient wilderness, minutes from the city

Hidden behind the towers of Shirokanedai lies the Institute for Nature Study — a rare surviving fragment of the old-growth Musashino forest, with roots in a villa garden first laid out in 1664. Most of Tokyo's greenery is planted and manicured. This is something else: a living woodland that has been left to grow, age, and tangle for centuries.

On this small-group walk we move slowly through old-growth woodlands, pause at quiet ponds and wetlands, and finish in a garden that preserves the native plants of the Musashino plain. Along the way I'll share the ecology behind what you're seeing — how an ancient forest persists inside one of the world's largest cities, and what the trees, water, and seasons can tell us.

It's part nature walk, part field lesson, part quiet escape.

The walk, step by step

Two hours and fifteen minutes in the forest

01

Meet & forest history

We gather at the entrance to the Institute for Nature Study and begin with the story of the 1664 Edo-era villa garden — how it became, by accident and intention, one of Tokyo's last old-growth forests.

02

Walk the old-growth woodlands

We follow the main path out from the Edo-era garden into mature woodland, stopping to read the forest: native species, ancient trees, and the quiet signs of how the ecosystem renews itself.

03

Wetlands & native plant garden

We reach the ponds and wetlands — home to waterbirds and seasonal wildlife — then move into a garden that preserves the native plants of the original Musashino plain.

04

Reflections & your questions

We close with time for your questions — about urban ecology, ancient trees, the seasons, or the way Japanese gardens hold nature. Bring your curiosity; this is what I do for a living.

Pace is gentle and the route is mostly flat, gravel paths — comfortable for anyone who enjoys an easy stroll.

Through the seasons

The forest never looks the same twice

Lush green in summer, irises over the wetlands in early summer, and the famous autumn illumination when the maples are lit after dark.

Your guide

Hi, I'm Yutaro

I'm a forest ecology researcher and a graduate of Minerva University. My work spans biodiversity fieldwork, the study of ancient trees, and environmental conservation using satellite data — but my real fascination is a simple question: how does nature survive inside a giant city?

The Institute for Nature Study is the perfect place to explore that. I love showing visitors the ecology hidden in plain sight here, and offering an ecologist's perspective on how Japanese gardens hold and shape the natural world.

I guide in English and Japanese, in small groups, at a relaxed pace. Whether you're a serious naturalist or just want a calm, beautiful morning away from the crowds, you're very welcome.

Questions

Frequently asked

Is the walk suitable for non-Japanese speakers?

Absolutely. I guide fluently in both English and Japanese, and the experience is designed with international visitors in mind.

How physically demanding is it?

Not at all demanding. We walk at a gentle pace on mostly flat gravel paths over about two hours, with plenty of stops. If you can enjoy a relaxed stroll in a park, you'll be comfortable.

What happens if it rains?

Light rain can be beautiful in the forest, so we usually go ahead with umbrellas. In the event of severe weather we'll reschedule or refund — just let me know.

Is there an admission fee to the Institute?

The Institute for Nature Study charges a small separate admission at the gate. I'll share current details when we confirm your booking.

Can I book a private group?

Yes — private groups are welcome, with a minimum of ¥30,000, for up to 10 guests. Great for families, friends, or special occasions.

When is the best season to visit?

Every season has its character: fresh green and irises in early summer, deep shade in midsummer, and spectacular autumn colour — including evening illumination — in late autumn. Tell me what you're hoping for and I'll advise.
